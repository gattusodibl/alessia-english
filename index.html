Mündliche Prüfung – Vorbereitung

Follow the Rabbit-Proof Fence

Doris Pilkington Garimara  ·  Alle Kapitel  ·  Englisch 7. Schuljahr

⚡ Quick Summary — The Whole Story in 2 Minutes

In one sentence

"Three Aboriginal girls are forcibly taken from their families in 1931 and walk over 1,000 miles through the Australian outback to get back home — guided by a fence."

The Background (Chapters 1–4)

From 1826 onwards, British colonisers arrive in Australia and gradually destroy Aboriginal life — taking land, banning ceremonies, punishing Aboriginal people under British law. By 1900, the Aboriginal Mardu people have lost almost everything. They relocate to a government outpost called Jigalong, navigating by a 1,200-mile rabbit-proof fence built in 1907.

The Removal (Chapter 5)

At Jigalong, Molly Craig (14) grows up with her two cousins Gracie and Daisy. All three are biracial — Aboriginal mother, white father. Under Australia's 1905 Aborigines Act, the government removes biracial children from their families to "educate" them into white society. In July 1931, Constable Riggs takes the three girls to the Moore River Native Settlement — hundreds of miles away.

The Settlement (Chapters 6–7)

Moore River is more like a prison than a school: padlocked dormitories, insect-infested food, a punishment cell called the "boob", and only English allowed. On her second night, Molly hears the bolt lock on her door — and decides: they must escape.

The Trek Home (Chapters 8–9)

Escaping is easy. Getting home is not. The girls walk for ~7 weeks, covering over 1,000 miles. They beg for food, dodge police trackers, and carry Daisy when her feet become infected. Near the end, Gracie leaves to find her own mother — and is recaptured. In October 1931, Molly and Daisy finally reach Jigalong. The key: Molly's father was a fence inspector — she knew to follow the fence north.

Epilogue

Molly is later taken back to Moore River after an illness. She escapes again — but her daughter Doris (age 4) is left behind. Doris grows up, becomes a journalist, and writes this book decades later to honour her mother's story.

The 3 things to always remember

🔑 The fence = built by colonisers, but becomes Molly's path to freedom

🔑 The removal policy = called "protection", was actually cultural destruction

🔑 Doris wrote this book = the story is an act of memory and resistance

Chapter-by-Chapter Summary

Introduction — The Author's Note
Who wrote this book, and how?
▶

The book is written by Doris Pilkington Garimara, whose Aboriginal name is Nugi Garimara. Doris is the daughter of Molly — one of the three girls at the centre of the story. She wrote this book to honour her mother's extraordinary journey.

Doris explains how she researched the story: she walked the same paths in her memory, studied the geography and plants of Western Australia, and interviewed her mother and aunt. One challenge: Aboriginal storytelling does not use Western dates and numbers in the same way. Time is linked to the seasons and the natural world, not to calendars. So the book is told in an unconventional, non-linear way — the reader must piece the timeline together.

Why this matters

The unusual structure of the book is intentional. Pilkington Garimara tells the story the way Aboriginal people would — connected to nature and memory, not to dates. This itself is a statement: Aboriginal culture is different from European culture, and neither is less valid.

Chapter 1 — The First Military Post
~1826 · The British arrive in King George Sound
▶

We meet Kundilla, the leader of the Nyungar tribe. It is early morning, around 1830. He has already been awake for hours, checking his fish traps in the river. About 60 members of his extended family are camped together for a coastal hunting season — a time of abundance and community.

Suddenly, a loud cannon boom rings out and terrifies the camp. Kundilla gathers the men to investigate. He recalls with dread the recent raids by white strangers — pirates, hunters, escaped convicts — who had come ashore, kidnapped Aboriginal women and taken them as slaves. The Nyungar men could not fight back against guns. They had little resistance anyway, because they believed the white men were gengas — spirits of their own ancestors returned from the dead. How could you fight your own ancestors?

The cannon boom is a salute: British soldiers under Major Edmund Lockyer are raising the Union Jack on the shore — establishing a military base at King George Sound (near what is now Albany) in 1826. When Kundilla approaches, he sees that these white men are different: they are not raiders. This outpost was actually set up to protect the Aboriginal people from rogue sailors.

Deeper meaning

The contrast is sharp: peaceful birdsong and rippling water vs. the boom of a cannon. The author uses these sounds deliberately. The cannon is a symbol of everything that is coming: the destruction of Aboriginal life. Kundilla represents all Aboriginal people — wise, peaceful, deeply connected to nature, about to have their world taken away.

Chapter 2 — The Swan River Colony
1829 · Britain claims Western Australia
▶

Two Nyungar brothers are out hunting when they are stopped by another leader, Yellagonga, who warns them about strange "gengas" nearby. These turn out to be British officers led by Captain Fremantle.

In one of the most striking scenes of the book, Fremantle speaks to the Nyungar people in English — a language they do not understand at all — and pretends to ask for their "approval" before naming their land Western Australia. The Nyungar understand nothing. Fremantle takes their silence and confused looks as "consent" and claims one million square miles of Aboriginal land in the name of King George IV.

A few weeks later, the first British settlers arrive from England. They are completely unprepared: they bring pianos, fine china, and fancy clothes — utterly useless for life in the Australian wilderness. They have no idea what they are walking into.

Deeper meaning

The "consent" scene is deeply ironic and painful. The British use language as a weapon of deception — not consciously cruel, but blind to the reality of the people they are dealing with. The settlers' ridiculous luggage (pianos!) shows how Eurocentric and ignorant they are. They assume Australia should just become England.

Chapter 3 — The Decline of Aboriginal Society
1800s · Culture under attack
▶

As more British settlers arrive, the damage to Aboriginal life becomes devastating. Their ancient hunting trails are blocked by British fences. Their land — which they have lived on for thousands of years — is taken over by farms and ranches. Since they can no longer hunt freely, they begin helping themselves to the settlers' sheep and cattle out of hunger.

The British punish them harshly. Aboriginal men who take livestock are arrested under British law, chained, and transported to island prison colonies. Meanwhile, when settlers kidnap Aboriginal women or commit other crimes, they go unpunished. A white man who kidnaps an Aboriginal man's wife is offered a bag of flour as "compensation." The Nyungar people cannot understand the injustice.

They now realise the white men are not spirits — they are ordinary humans. But they still cannot understand why they are being treated this way.

Deeper meaning

The author's point is clear: the British do not consider Aboriginal people to be fully human. This is not just racism — it is dehumanisation. A woman's kidnapping is worth a bag of flour. The chapter ends with a disturbing real quote from a British novelist (1873) who said Aboriginal people "have to go." This shows how widespread and accepted these attitudes were.

Chapter 4 — From the Deserts They Came
Early 1900s · The Mardu journey to Jigalong
▶

By 1900, Western Australia is prospering — for white people. The Aboriginal Mardu people have lost almost everything: their land, their hunting grounds, their culture. They realise their spears and boomerangs are useless against guns. They make a fateful decision: to relocate.

Their journey takes them across the Great Sandy Desert. Along the way, they encounter a horse for the first time in their lives — ridden by a biracial man named Harry Phillips, who turns out to be friendly and leads them to the station at Talawana. Eventually the group continues east, finds the rabbit-proof fence, and follows it to the government outpost at Jigalong.

Here the author explains the fence: it was built in 1907 to stop rabbits from spreading across Western Australia. It stretches nearly 1,200 miles from north to south. It completely failed to stop rabbits (they just dug under it) — but it became an invaluable navigation tool for Aboriginal people. Find the fence, and you know where you are.

Maude — who will later become Molly's mother — is born and grows up happily at Jigalong.

Deeper meaning

The author compares the Mardu's journey to the biblical story of the Israelites: a people without a homeland, searching for safety. The fence — built by the British to protect their farms — ironically becomes the Aboriginal people's lifeline. Later, it becomes Molly's path home.

Chapter 5 — Jigalong, 1907–1931
Molly is born · The removal policy · The girls are taken
▶

Jigalong is a government depot for the men who maintain the rabbit-proof fence. Aboriginal families settle nearby, living seminomadically — hunting and gathering, but also receiving food and supplies from the depot. Life is not comfortable, but it is a community.

Maude, now 16, learns her arranged husband no longer wants her. She is secretly relieved. But then a white fence inspector gets her pregnant. When her daughter is born, the father names her Molly. She is the first officially recorded biracial child at Jigalong.

Molly grows into a lively, bright girl — but the other Aboriginal children refuse to play with her because her skin is lighter. She is caught between two worlds. When she is four, two more biracial girls arrive at Jigalong: her half-sister Gracie and her younger cousin Daisy. The three girls become inseparable.

The depot superintendent is worried: the three girls are being excluded by the other children. He writes to the government, saying they are "not getting a fair chance." Then a white woman writes to Mr. Neville, Chief Protector of Aborigines, complaining that the girls are "running wild with the whites." This is enough. Under Australia's 1905 Aborigines Act, the government has the legal right to remove biracial children from their families by force.

One day, Constable Riggs arrives and takes Molly (14), Gracie, and Daisy away to the Moore River Native Settlement. Their families are powerless. Gracie's mother shouts at Gracie's white father for not stopping it. He says interfering would have been illegal — but this is cold comfort.

Deeper meaning

The government's logic was racist but hidden behind "care": they believed biracial children were smarter than full-Aboriginal children and should be "educated" into white society. In reality, the goal was to make Aboriginal culture disappear within a generation. This policy is known today as the Stolen Generations — one of the darkest chapters in Australian history.

Chapters 6–7 — The Journey South & Moore River
August 1931 · The settlement revealed
▶

The girls travel south in a long series of short trips and overnight stays. Along the way they meet two older Aboriginal girls who are excited about their futures at the settlement — dreams that will never come true. Those girls will be sent to work as servants on farms and won't see their families for years.

Molly, Gracie, and Daisy arrive at the Moore River Native Settlement in August 1931. They arrive at dusk and notice no one is outside. Then they see why: the residents have already been locked into their padlocked dormitories for the night. The girls are shown to a cold room with hard mattresses and only one thin blanket each.

The next morning is worse. Breakfast is a thin, insect-infested porridge. An older girl named Martha Jones takes them on a tour. Molly thinks it looks exactly like a prison. She sees the "boob" — the punishment cell where disobedient children are locked up for a week. She learns that only English is allowed — speaking their own Aboriginal language is forbidden.

That night, lying in her locked dormitory, Molly hears the bolt slide shut. In that moment she makes her decision: they must escape.

Deeper meaning

The settlement is presented as a prison, not a school. The details — locks, thin food, punishment cells, forbidden language — are all real historical facts. Banning their language is especially significant: language carries culture, identity, and memory. To ban it is to erase who they are.

Chapters 8–9 — The Escape & The Trek Home
August–October 1931 · 1,000 miles on foot
▶

Escaping from Moore River is easy — the girls simply slip off the school grounds on their second day. Finding their way home is the real challenge.

Molly knows that her father was a fence inspector. If they can find the rabbit-proof fence, they can follow it north all the way home to Jigalong. But reaching the fence takes days of exhausting walking through unfamiliar country.

The girls try to catch their own food — lizards, berries — but it is too unreliable. They begin knocking on farmhouse doors to beg for food, knowing that any farmer could report them to the police. They keep walking.

News of the escape makes the newspapers. Authorities send a tracker — a man skilled at following footprints — to recapture them. The girls have several terrifyingly close calls, hiding in the bush as searchers pass within metres.

Finally they reach the fence. But they are only halfway — there are still 500 miles to go. As weeks pass, Daisy's feet become badly infected with deep scratches and wounds. Molly and Gracie must take turns carrying her on their backs.

Then, not far from the end, Gracie makes a devastating choice. She hears that her mother is at a nearby station and decides she cannot walk any further. She leaves Molly and Daisy and goes to find her mother. She is quickly caught by police and taken back to Moore River. She will never return to Jigalong.

In October 1931, after roughly seven weeks of walking, Molly and Daisy arrive home. Their relatives are stunned — they cannot believe what the girls have done. The authorities hear where they are but decide not to pursue them: they have spent too much money already.

Deeper meaning

The trek is both a physical and spiritual journey. Molly's knowledge — inherited from her father — saves them. It is also a symbol: the fence, built by the colonisers, becomes the path to freedom. Gracie's departure is heartbreaking: it shows how close the girls come to breaking, and makes Molly's determination all the more remarkable.

Epilogue — What Happened Next
The rest of their lives
▶

Molly marries a stockman and has two daughters: Doris (the author) and Annabelle. When the girls are very young, Molly gets appendicitis. The moment she leaves hospital, she and her daughters are returned to Moore River. Molly escapes again — this time with only her younger daughter. Four-year-old Doris is left behind. She will not see her mother again for decades. Annabelle is later taken from Molly and put in a children's home — Molly never sees her again.

Gracie never returns to Jigalong. She finishes at Moore River, works on various farms, marries a station hand, and has six children.

Daisy moves to another camp with her family, trains as a housemaid, marries, and has four children.

Doris remains at Moore River until age 12, then goes to a Christian mission. She refuses to become a servant. She trains as a nurse's aide, marries, has four children. In the 1960s she takes her children to meet Molly for the first time. Later she studies journalism, researches Aboriginal history, and writes three books — including this one.

Deeper meaning

The epilogue shows that the story did not end with Molly's return home. The government's policies kept tearing her family apart. And yet: Doris writes this book. She turns her family's suffering into history, into literature, into something the world can read and remember. That is its own form of resistance.

Key Characters

Molly Craig
Protagonist · Age 14

Brave, determined, and a natural leader. She plans the escape and never gives up, even when carrying Daisy on her back. Her father's job as a fence inspector gives her the crucial knowledge to navigate home. She is a symbol of resilience and love for family.

Gracie Fields
Molly's cousin

Younger and less resilient than Molly. She makes it most of the way home, but near the end she cannot continue and leaves to find her own mother. She is quickly recaptured. Her choice is heartbreaking but understandable — she had reached her limit.

Daisy Kadibil
The youngest girl

The most vulnerable of the three. Her feet become so badly infected she must be carried. Despite this, she endures. She eventually marries and lives a settled life — the most "normal" outcome of the three girls.

Maude (Molly's mother)
Molly's Aboriginal mother

A 16-year-old Aboriginal girl at Jigalong. She has an affair with Thomas Craig and raises Molly until the government takes her away. Powerless to stop the removal, her grief drives the emotional weight of the departure scene.

Thomas Craig
Molly's white father

A rabbit-proof fence inspector. Almost absent from the story — yet crucial. The knowledge he passed on (directly or indirectly) to Molly about the fence is the reason she can navigate home. Without him, there is no escape route.

Constable Riggs
The government officer

The policeman who removes the girls from Jigalong. He is not portrayed as a monster — he is doing his job under the law. That is what makes him so disturbing: he represents a system, not just one cruel person.

Mr. Neville
Chief Protector of Aborigines

The government official responsible for the removal policy. He genuinely believed he was helping biracial children. His view: they were "superior" to full-blooded Aboriginal people and should be educated into white society. This is racist paternalism — controlling people "for their own good."

Doris Pilkington Garimara
The author · Molly's daughter

Separated from her mother at age 4 when Molly escapes Moore River the second time. She grows up at the settlement. Decades later, she becomes a journalist and researcher, and writes this book as an act of love and historical memory.

Kundilla & Yellagonga
Tribal leaders (Chapters 1–2)

Aboriginal leaders who appear in the historical opening chapters, set in the 1820s–30s. They represent the original Aboriginal world — peaceful, connected to nature, and utterly unprepared for what the British will bring.

Themes & Messages

Theme 1

Freedom & Resilience

The central theme. Molly's decision to escape is not impulsive — she observes the settlement carefully, understands the danger, and acts deliberately. The 1,000-mile trek is the most powerful image in the book: three girls, on foot, against the entire Australian government. Molly never gives up, even when she must carry Daisy on her back with her own feet bleeding.

Example to use: "The theme of resilience is shown when Molly and Gracie carry Daisy on their backs, even though their own feet are also wounded. Molly keeps going because she knows that giving up means never seeing her family again."

Theme 2

Racism & Colonialism

The book traces a direct line from the first British arrival (1826) to the forced removal of children (1931). Colonialism did not just take land — it took culture, language, identity, and children. The government's removal policy was based on a racist belief: that Aboriginal people were inferior, and that biracial children could be "saved" by becoming white. The book names this clearly and refuses to soften it.

Example: "The government took the girls because of racist beliefs — they thought biracial children should be separated from Aboriginal culture. This is an example of colonialism destroying families."

Theme 3

Identity & Belonging

The three girls exist between worlds: too light-skinned to be fully accepted by the Aboriginal children, but taken away precisely because they are not fully white. Molly's trek home is also a journey to reclaim who she is. The settlement tries to replace her language, her culture, her family — her whole identity. She refuses.

Example: "Molly's identity is connected to Jigalong, her family, and her culture. The settlement tried to erase that identity by forbidding her language and separating her from her mother. Her escape is a refusal to let that happen."

Theme 4

Family & Connection to Country

For Aboriginal people, "country" is not just a place — it is a spiritual and emotional home. The land, the ancestors, the community are all connected. The girls are not just walking home; they are walking back to everything that makes them who they are. The rabbit-proof fence — a symbol of colonial control — becomes the path that leads them back to their roots.

Example: "The rabbit-proof fence is a symbol in the book. It was built by the British, but Molly uses it to find her way home. This shows how even colonial structures can be turned into tools of resistance."

Theme 5

History & Memory

The book itself is an act of remembering. Doris writes it decades after the events, based on interviews with her mother and aunt. Aboriginal storytelling does not use dates — it uses landscape, seasons, and memory. The unusual structure of the book (non-linear, jumping in time) reflects this. The author is saying: this is how we remember. This is our history, told our way.

Example: "Doris wrote this book to preserve her mother's story. By telling it in the Aboriginal storytelling tradition — without strict dates and chronology — she is also celebrating Aboriginal culture."

💡 Exam tip: Always connect a theme to a specific moment in the book. Examiners love examples. Use the phrases: "This is shown when…", "For example…", "This is significant because…"

Important Vocabulary

half-caste / biracial
A person with one Aboriginal and one white European parent. Used as an official term by the government — today considered offensive.
Aboriginal
The indigenous (native) people of Australia, living there for 50,000+ years before the British arrived.
Stolen Generations
The historical policy of forcibly removing Aboriginal and biracial children from their families — Australia officially apologised in 2008.
gengas
The Aboriginal Nyungar word for spirits of ancestors. They initially believed the white men were gengas — which is why they didn't resist.
rabbit-proof fence
A ~1,200-mile fence built in 1907 to stop rabbits from spreading west. It failed as a rabbit barrier but became a navigation tool for Aboriginal people.
Moore River Settlement
A government institution where biracial Aboriginal children were sent to be "educated" into white society. In reality it was more like a prison.
the "boob"
The punishment cell at Moore River. Children could be locked there for up to a week for disobedience.
Protector of Aborigines
An official government role — supposedly to protect Aboriginal people. In reality, these officials enforced removal policies.
colonialism
When one country takes over another's land and people, imposing its own laws and culture. Britain colonised Australia from 1788.
seminomadic
Moving between places seasonally rather than staying in one permanent location. Traditional Aboriginal lifestyle.
depot / station
A depot is a supply/maintenance base. A station (in Australia) is a very large ranch, often thousands of square miles.
tracker
A person skilled at following footprints and signs in nature. The authorities used a tracker to try to find the escaped girls.
trek
A long, difficult journey on foot.
dormitory
A large shared bedroom. At Moore River, dormitories were locked and bolted at night.
